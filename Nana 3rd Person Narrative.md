# **The Life and Times of Nana: An Engineer's Journey**

## **Foreword**

This is the story of an engineer whose career spanned some of India's most critical industrial development years. From the hallowed halls of BITS Pilani in the late 1950s to the workshops of Bharat Heavy Electricals Limited, his journey reflects the dedication, ingenuity, and work ethic that built modern India's engineering foundation.

---

## **Chapter 1: The College Years at BITS Pilani (1958-1959)**

In 1958, a young man arrived at the Birla Institute of Technology and Science in Pilani, ready to embark on his engineering education. This was before the India-China War, a time when the nation was still finding its industrial footing, and institutions like BITS Pilani were grooming the engineers who would build the country's future.

The campus sprawled across the Rajasthani landscape, with its distinctive architecture centered around a prominent clock tower. On either side of a central green lawn stood two main buildings—the engineering college on the left and the arts and science college on the right. Between them, a magnificent library occupied one side of the tower, with a great hall on the other. The campus was meticulously maintained, with lush green grounds that students were required to parade across on national holidays—January 26th and August 15th. Participation in these parades was compulsory for all students.

He completed his engineering degree in 1959, graduating into a world that was rapidly industrializing. The education was rigorous, practical, and demanding. At BITS Pilani, students didn't just learn theory—they worked with their hands. The training school required students to spend at least one year working on various machines, learning the fundamentals of mechanical operations. Overalls were compulsory for everyone during these practical sessions, as they learned to operate lathes, milling machines, and drilling equipment.

The principal at Maharaja College, where he also studied, was extremely strict but had a unique gift for teaching. He taught mathematics, and his classes, which took place during the last period of the day around 2 PM (college ran until 5 PM), were unlike any other. Students would laugh continuously throughout his lectures. He had a remarkable method of teaching mathematics through humor and engagement, making even the most complex concepts accessible. He would write a problem on the board, teach for a while, then ask questions about what should go where in an integration problem. The figure would already be given, and students would respond, everyone learning together, including the professor himself. It was an excellent method that made mathematics memorable and enjoyable.

## **Chapter 2: Hostel Life and Brotherhood**

Life in the hostel at BITS Pilani was an experience that shaped character and built lifelong friendships. Approximately 144 students lived together in his hostel, sharing meals, studying, and navigating the challenges of communal living.

The rooms were simple—austere, even by the standards of the day. Each room measured roughly 8 feet by 8 feet and was divided from the adjacent room by a wire mesh wall, allowing sound to carry between them. The furnishings were minimal: a wooden cot (takht) with no mattress, a bookshelf, a table, and a chair. That was all the furniture provided by the institution. Students would place a dari (mat) on the wooden plank, followed by a thin covering, and sleep on that. Bed bugs were a constant companion, though nobody seemed to mind much at the time.

The hostel had a common room where students gathered to play carrom, chess, and table tennis. Interestingly, bridge was not played in the common room because too many "advisors" would gather around, offering unsolicited guidance. When someone started a chess game between two players, advisors would appear, and eventually, the original players would simply get up and leave for table tennis instead. The table tennis matches often became "shooting" sessions against whoever was the best player—a way to challenge and improve skills.

Some students had particularly strong bonds. If someone received a care package of snacks from home, everyone would gather in that person's room and finish everything together. This sense of sharing and community was fundamental to hostel life.

There were legendary eaters among them. One student in particular was famous for his appetite. During special Sunday meals at the mess, when potato parathas with yogurt and rajma (kidney beans) were served, this student once ate 30 potato parathas in a single sitting—a hostel record. The general potato consumption per person averaged about half a kilogram. When fried potatoes were served, they came heaped on large plates, and one person could finish an entire plate alone. Vegetables weren't particularly popular among the students, but potatoes were universally beloved.

The hostel also had its share of memorable characters. There was one fellow whose appetite was so legendary that when he sat down to eat at any table, the other students would immediately get up and leave, knowing they wouldn't get any food otherwise. He would take too long eating, consuming everything in sight.

## **Chapter 3: The Mess Hall: A Study in Logistics and Appetite**

The mess hall was a marvel of efficiency and organization, feeding around 300 people in just half an hour. The dining tables were long wooden affairs with benches on both sides, each seating approximately 15-16 people. Food was served in pressed metal thalis—the special railway-style plates with three compartments in front, two in back, and one in the middle for rotis.

The food service itself was a well-oiled machine. The person serving would bring a stack of these pressed thalis and start distributing them. The first person at the table would receive their thali and pass it along, making the process remarkably fast. The rotis were made on a massive tawa (griddle) measuring about 4 feet (approximately 2.5 meters) in diameter. One expert would roll the rotis, another would place them on the tawa, and a third would flip the cooked rotis and stamp them with a cloth dipped in ghee.

Some students were famous for their enormous appetites. While most ate around 15-20 rotis per meal, occasionally someone would reach counts of up to 100\. The dining system accommodated this remarkable consumption efficiently.

One particular tradition involved ghee. Some students would bring their own ghee, heat it up, and add it to the dal. Onions in large bowls were kept on the tables for anyone who wanted them.

Sunday mornings featured special meals, including the famous laddoos made from besan (gram flour). These weren't ordinary laddoos—they were massive, with a diameter of about three inches. Students called them "brahmachari laddoos." When important visitors like the Birlas would visit the campus, the meals would become even more special, with better quality food served.

One memorable incident involved mangoes. A group of students had eaten mangoes and kept the pits, which still had some flesh on them. They decided to play a prank—they blew air into the mango pits and collected 101 of them, placing them on a table. When people arrived and saw what appeared to be fresh, good mangoes on display, they rushed to grab them, only to discover the deception. It became a legendary campus joke.

The mess experience taught valuable lessons about logistics, efficiency, and the importance of good food in keeping a community together. Years later, reflecting on college life, he would note that food was one of the primary things people remembered from their college days—after all, it's difficult to have the worst food possible so that you remember it for the rest of your life.

## **Chapter 4: Sports and Physical Training**

Sports were not optional at BITS Pilani—they were mandatory. Every evening, students were required to participate in sports activities. This was a time when physical fitness was considered essential to a well-rounded education.

Before arriving at Pilani, he had played various sports at home, including hockey and cricket. Sometimes they would use fruit as a ball, other times a normal ball. When he reached Jaipur for college, he tried out for hockey at Maharaja College but didn't make the team. So he switched to volleyball instead.

His volleyball serve was legendary—so powerful that if it hit someone on the head, the person would be knocked out. This actually happened once or twice during games. He and his friends formed the A and B teams of the college, though they weren't part of the official teams. They played purely for the love of the sport.

At Pilani, sports continued to be an important part of daily life. The campus even had the privilege of hosting Major Dhyan Chand, the legendary hockey player, who came to teach hockey techniques to interested students. This was a remarkable opportunity that left a lasting impression.

For those who didn't play hockey, volleyball was the popular choice. Cricket was also played, though it wasn't as famous at that time as it would later become. Students would play with their own wooden bats and whatever equipment they could improvise.

The Army Cadet Corps (ACC) also had programs in the evening, and while participation was encouraged, many students tried to avoid it when possible, preferring their sports activities instead.

## **Chapter 5: Beginning a Career at Bharat Heavy Electricals Limited**

After graduating from BITS Pilani in 1959, he joined Bharat Heavy Electricals Limited (BHL) in Bhopal. This marked the beginning of a distinguished career in India's heavy engineering sector. BHL was one of India's flagship public sector enterprises, manufacturing power plant equipment, transformers, and other heavy electrical machinery.

The early days at BHL involved extensive training. Fresh graduates were put through rigorous programs to learn practical applications of their theoretical knowledge. The training school focused heavily on hands-on work with machinery, reinforcing the practical skills that had been introduced during college.

Initially, he lived in the hostel for two years. During this time, hostel residents took turns serving as mess manager for one month each. This role involved ordering vegetables and managing supplies. Once, someone asked who had ordered matches, and everyone denied it. The reason was simple: in the kitchen, the wood fire was kept burning continuously—it never went out—so matches were never actually needed. This was the level of efficiency and continuous operation maintained in the facilities.

After the hostel, he was allocated a small one-room apartment. Several bachelors living nearby pooled resources and hired a cook together. The cook would prepare meals in his room, and everyone would gather there to eat. The furniture situation was minimal—they purchased a small study table (which still exists in the family today) and two chairs. For additional seating during meals, they would flip empty oil drums upside down and sit on those.

The cook was essential to their survival, and when he occasionally left, chaos would ensue. Once, when the cook went away, five or six of them attempted to make khichdi (a rice and lentil dish) with disastrous results. When the cook returned another time at 10-11 PM at night, everyone was overjoyed. They had been playing bridge while waiting, nobody thinking about food anymore. When he arrived, they immediately had him cook a proper meal at 11 PM, and then ate contentedly. Such was the dependence on having someone who knew how to cook properly.

One particularly memorable incident involved an apocalyptic prediction that was circulating at the time—some prophecy that the world was going to end. The response among the group was pragmatic and very much in character: "Fine, let it end. But today, let's eat good food." They had an excellent meal that day, and of course, nothing happened. The world continued as usual.

## **Chapter 6: The Arjun Tank Project**

One of the most significant projects of his career was his involvement with India's Arjun Main Battle Tank program. This indigenous tank development project was a matter of national pride and strategic importance, and he played a crucial role as the coordinator for the assembly of the tank at BHL Bhopal.

The project was massive in scope. He had to review and work with approximately 10,000 drawings. Every single drawing had to be examined for materials, inspection procedures, and testing protocols. Some components weighed as little as 50 grams, while others were massive steel plates that arrived from Rourkela Steel Plant. His job was to coordinate with inspection teams and testing personnel to ensure that proper materials were used and that inspection could be conducted effectively on each component.

The components came from various sources. Large steel pieces arrived from other facilities, and some machining was done at BHL for assembly purposes. At the facility, approximately 100 small sub-assemblies were manufactured, each consisting of 10-15 to 20-25 parts.

He was essentially in charge of the mechanical parts of the prototype assembly at BHL, while other major assemblies like the engine came from external sources, some imported from Germany. In total, they assembled two prototype tanks at the facility.

During this work, he discovered numerous design flaws and manufacturing issues. The developers had not selected proper nuts and bolts—many would come loose and fall off due to vibrations. When he raised this issue with the concerned designer at CVRDE (Combat Vehicles Research and Development Establishment), he provided them with technical references from books, explaining that the selected fasteners would definitely come loose under vibration. The solution required using fine threads and special washers that would lock the movement of the bolts.

One particularly troubling discovery was that the same tank had three different designs for a single common component. When a Brigadier who was in charge of assembly visited, he carefully framed his concerns as questions rather than criticisms: "Sir, how do you manage this? You've installed three different components with the same application." The answer came through action—the Brigadier brought about 50 people to the facility and had all the components changed to a single type.

The tank's tracks also lacked proper locking mechanisms. When asked how they locked the tracks, the developers eventually changed the entire track design, importing new ones with proper locking arrangements.

Another major concern was weight. The Arjun tank was significantly heavier than comparable tanks. At one seminar at NDA Khadakwasla, he attended a presentation on the tank. He noticed that other attendees were asking basic questions, and realized he could ask much better, more technical questions. During discussions, he pointed out that they were using steel with excessive thickness tolerances. By reducing the tolerances, they could reduce the weight of each plate, and with thousands of plates in a tank, the cumulative weight savings would be substantial.

The developers reported daily deviations in thickness—some plates were 4mm thicker than specified, others 5mm over. He told them to use the plates as they were, since the extra thickness actually provided additional protection. The challenge was getting access to the specifications, which were classified as secret documents. He explained that they would need written permission from their chairman to share such documents, and if the specifications leaked, it would create serious problems. This was sensitive, classified work.

He also raised practical operational questions: How would the tank perform in desert conditions at 70°C temperatures? When steel heats up in direct sunlight, the interior becomes extremely hot—just as a car's steering wheel becomes untouchable when left in the sun. The developers mentioned some air supply for comfort, and there was night vision capability for seeing enemies in the dark.

The engine compartment required special attention. The German engine that was supplied had to be modified to reduce weight. When German salespeople visited to discuss supply, he asked them two critical questions: How would their tank operate in desert areas? What would the engine's de-rating be when sand filled the available spaces? The de-rating could be 20-30%, rendering the tank much less effective.

He also questioned the cathodic protection for the tank—there seemed to be no locking mechanism for the bottom chain tracks, which would eventually come loose under field conditions.

After analyzing everything, he prepared a report for management showing that approximately 80% of the tank's cost was in materials, with only 20% for everything else. He presented his findings, but after his retirement, nobody pursued the project further at BHL.

Despite all these early challenges, the Arjun tank did eventually enter service with the Indian Army in a modified version. He hoped that they had addressed all the problems identified during those early development years. The project taught valuable lessons about the importance of proper design, quality control, and attention to detail in defense manufacturing.

## **Chapter 7: Traction Control Systems and Railway Engineering**

Another significant project involved traction control systems for Mumbai's suburban trains. This work combined his expertise in electrical systems, mechanical engineering, and practical problem-solving.

The suburban trains had three coaches, with one coach containing four traction motors underneath. The control system was managed by the driver, with a control room located behind the driver's compartment. High voltage systems were involved, so the equipment couldn't be opened casually by just anyone. If someone tried to open the case, the switches would automatically turn off in the off position, a critical safety feature.

At that time, the system used conventional control methods. Modern electronic controls hadn't yet been introduced—CNC machines didn't exist yet, as processors weren't available at that time. The motors were controlled through mechanical switching systems, with different contacts that would close and open to control the entire operation.

Testing the equipment was a major responsibility. The railways insisted that even though BHL manufactured to their designs, they needed type testing of each and every item because manufacturing defects could be catastrophic. With the available facilities, they conducted mechanical operation tests ranging from 1 million to 2,000 to 4,000 cycles, ensuring reliability.

This extensive testing regime meant he became deeply familiar with every aspect of the equipment. When someone reported a problem, he could diagnose and provide solutions immediately over the phone, telling them exactly what to do. This expertise came from hands-on experience with every component and system.

The work in railway traction systems was just one part of his broader portfolio at BHL, which included transformers, turbines, switch gear, and motors. The technology for motors came from Siemens, while transformer technology was British in origin.

## **Chapter 8: The Hazira Oil Pipeline and Cathodic Protection**

He also worked on the Hazira oil pipeline project, which involved cathodic protection systems. This was specialized work that protected pipeline infrastructure from corrosion—a critical concern for long-distance oil and gas pipelines.

Cathodic protection works on a simple but crucial principle: the metal pipeline and the surrounding earth both have electrical potential. If current flows from the pipe into the earth, metal erosion occurs. To prevent this, anodes are installed in the ground, which redirect the current flow away from the pipeline, protecting it from corrosion.

The Hazira pipeline ran from Gujarat all the way to Uttar Pradesh. All such oil pipelines required cathodic protection systems, which became standard practice for pipeline infrastructure across India.

When he was in Ankleshwar, the work involved a pipeline from the nearest jetty point to the refinery—approximately a mile of pipe. The first time he took a boat on the river to reach the jetty, it was intimidating, but he quickly became accustomed to the work environment. He oversaw the installation, worked with the staff, and ensured the cathodic protection system was properly implemented.

This type of work required understanding electrochemistry, corrosion science, electrical engineering, and practical field conditions. It was typical of the multidisciplinary problem-solving that characterized his career.

## **Chapter 9: International Experience in the United Kingdom**

His career also took him internationally. He spent six months in the United Kingdom working on traction control systems. This was a valuable experience that exposed him to different engineering approaches and international best practices.

In the UK, he met with various people in the industry. Some were excellent collaborators, while others weren't even prepared to talk. The development head from their organization visited Bhopal once, and they had productive discussions. One or two people who assisted with inspections became good friends during his time there, and when he visited the UK, he reconnected with them.

Overall, the UK experience was very positive and enriching. He learned different approaches to similar problems and saw how international companies managed quality control, testing, and manufacturing.

The work focused on traction control systems similar to what was being developed for Indian railways, but with different specifications and requirements. The exposure to international standards and practices proved valuable when he returned to India and continued his work.

## **Chapter 10: Engineering Philosophy and Problem-Solving Approach**

Throughout his career, certain principles guided his approach to engineering problems. These weren't just technical guidelines but a philosophy of work that ensured quality and reliability.

**Attention to Detail**: When examining the 10,000 drawings for the Arjun tank, he didn't just skim through them. Each drawing was carefully analyzed for materials, tolerances, inspection procedures, and testing requirements. This thoroughness revealed problems that others might have missed.

**Never Cut Corners**: One of his fundamental beliefs was that wherever corners were cut, failure would inevitably follow. In software development today, people often try to cut corners to meet deadlines, but as he observed, systems never work properly until they're fixed completely. It's not possible to just patch problems—you have to address root causes.

**Hands-On Experience**: His time working on lathes, milling machines, and drilling equipment wasn't just training—it gave him practical understanding of manufacturing tolerances, material properties, and what could realistically be achieved in production. This knowledge proved invaluable when reviewing designs and identifying potential manufacturing issues.

**Question Everything**: When developers presented their work, he would ask probing questions, not to criticize but to understand. How will this work in desert conditions? What happens when vibration occurs? What are the failure modes? These questions often revealed design flaws that needed addressing.

**Do the Job Right the First Time**: Whether it was selecting the correct nuts and bolts or ensuring proper stress-free assembly, his approach emphasized getting things right initially rather than fixing problems later. He observed that when components were forced into position during assembly, creating stress, they would inevitably fail under vibration conditions.

**Learn from Others' Mistakes**: He paid attention to examples from history, like when England changed from riveted to welded train frames—an improvement in design that actually created stress problems, causing widespread failures. These lessons informed his own work and made him cautious about changes that seemed like improvements but might introduce new problems.

**Maintain Professional Standards**: Even when dealing with classified defense projects, he maintained ethical boundaries. When asked to share secret specifications, he explained the proper procedures and why unauthorized disclosure would be problematic, demonstrating professional integrity.

## **Chapter 11: Life After BITS: Comparing Generations**

Later in life, when his grandchildren were preparing for college, he reflected on how education and student life had changed—and how much had stayed the same.

The basic hostel room structure remained remarkably consistent across decades. Whether in the 1950s or the 2020s, a hostel room typically contained a bed, a desk, and a storage cabinet. The room sizes might vary slightly, but the fundamental setup was unchanged. What had changed were the amenities—modern students might have mattresses instead of wooden planks, better bathrooms, and certainly better connectivity.

He recalled that his college had a particularly terrible cot—so uncomfortable that they removed the door from the built-in wall cabinet, brought some bricks, placed the door on the bricks, and slept on that instead. It made a more comfortable bed than the original furniture provided.

Cleanliness standards had evolved too. He candidly admitted that during his bachelor days in Pune, living in a room measuring about 10 by 12 feet, he didn't clean for six months. When someone finally came to sweep, the accumulation was extraordinary. But at the time, there was simply no thought process about surroundings—you didn't notice the dirt, didn't feel it under your feet. The focus was entirely on work and studies.

The food experiences, however, seemed universal. Whether at BITS in the 1950s or modern universities, students remembered the food—both the good meals and the terrible ones. The special Sunday meals with aloo parathas, the mess politics, the legendary eaters—these were experiences that transcended generations.

Sports requirements had changed. While sports were mandatory at Pilani, modern universities offered more choices and flexibility. But the underlying principle remained valuable—physical fitness as part of a complete education.

He observed his grandchildren considering colleges like UC Berkeley, Georgia Tech, and UC San Diego, comparing campuses, programs, and opportunities. These institutions had their own iconic features—clock towers seemed to be universal across campuses, much like at BITS Pilani. Every college seemed to need one, perhaps because in earlier times, not everyone had watches, so a central clock tower helped everyone synchronize their time.

When his grandchildren showed him pictures of BITS Pilani's modern campus, he recognized the central tower with the library beneath it, but noted that the middle portion had changed. The ground floor area looked different from his time. The campus had expanded significantly, with new hostels built wherever space could be found.

The fundamental challenge of student life remained constant: adjusting to all kinds of people, maintaining cleanliness under academic pressure, managing time between studies and recreation, and learning to live independently. Whether in 1958 or 2025, these were the formative experiences of college life.

## **Chapter 12: Legacy of an Engineer**

His career at Bharat Heavy Electricals Limited spanned critical decades in India's industrial development. From power plant equipment to defense systems, from railway infrastructure to pipeline protection, his work touched multiple sectors of India's growing industrial base.

The Arjun tank project, despite its challenges and his eventual retirement before its completion, represented the ambition of indigenous defense manufacturing. His meticulous documentation of problems, his technical analysis, and his recommendations contributed to the knowledge base that would eventually lead to a functional Indian main battle tank.

The traction control systems he worked on kept Mumbai's suburban trains running reliably, transporting millions of commuters. The cathodic protection systems safeguarded thousands of kilometers of oil pipelines, ensuring energy security. The transformers, turbines, and switchgear manufactured and tested under his oversight powered India's electrical grid.

But beyond the specific projects, his legacy lay in his approach to engineering: thorough, ethical, practical, and uncompromising on quality. He demonstrated that cutting corners led to failure, that proper design required understanding real-world conditions, and that hands-on experience was invaluable for making sound decisions.

His generation of engineers built India's industrial infrastructure with limited resources, adapted foreign technology to Indian conditions, and trained the next generation of engineers. They worked without modern computers, without CNC machines, without email, but with deep technical knowledge, practical skills, and dedication to quality.

The stories of hostel life, mess hall meals, sports activities, and pranks show the human side of this engineering journey—the friendships, the humor, the shared struggles of student life that created bonds lasting decades. These experiences shaped not just technical skills but character, resilience, and the ability to work with diverse people toward common goals.

From the wooden cots at BITS Pilani to the assembly floor of India's main battle tank, from volleyball courts to traction control systems, his journey reflects a generation that transformed India from a newly independent nation into an industrial power. Their work laid the foundation for everything that followed.

## **Epilogue: The Importance of Memory**

In conversations with his grandchildren, comparing his college experiences at BITS Pilani with their modern university options, he emphasized certain unchanging truths: the importance of hands-on learning, the value of paying attention to details, the necessity of doing things right the first time, and the bonds formed through shared experiences.

He spoke of legendary professors who made mathematics enjoyable, of friends who became lifelong companions, of meals that fed both body and spirit, of projects that tested skills and character. These weren't just stories of the past—they were lessons for the future.

The transcript of these conversations, originally recorded casually during a family video call, captures not just historical facts but wisdom earned through decades of engineering practice. It documents a time when students lived simply but learned deeply, when resources were limited but ingenuity was boundless, when tolerance levels had to be precise to the millimeter but human connections were warm and lasting.

This is the story of Nana—grandfather, engineer, problem-solver, mentor. His life reminds us that behind every transformer humming in a power station, every train running on schedule, every pipeline safely transporting fuel, there are engineers who sweated the details, questioned assumptions, and refused to compromise on quality.

Their legacy continues in the infrastructure we use daily, in the standards they established, and in the example they set for engineering excellence.
